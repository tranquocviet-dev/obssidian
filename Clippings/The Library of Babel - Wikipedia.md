"**The Library of Babel**" ([Spanish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_language "Spanish language"): *La biblioteca de Babel*) is a [short story](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Short_story "Short story") by [Argentine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argentina "Argentina") author and librarian [Jorge Luis Borges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jorge_Luis_Borges "Jorge Luis Borges") (1899–1986), conceiving of a universe in the form of a vast library containing all possible 410-page books of a certain format and [character set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Character_set "Character set").

The story was originally published in Spanish in Borges' [1941](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1941_in_literature "1941 in literature") collection of stories *[El jardín de senderos que se bifurcan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Garden_of_Forking_Paths "The Garden of Forking Paths")* (*The Garden of Forking Paths*). That entire book was, in turn, included within his much-reprinted *[Ficciones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ficciones "Ficciones")* ([1944](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1944_in_literature "1944 in literature")). Two [English-language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_language "English language") translations appeared approximately simultaneously in [1962](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1962_in_literature "1962 in literature"), one by James E. Irby in a diverse collection of Borges's works titled *[Labyrinths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Labyrinths_\(short_story_collection\) "Labyrinths (short story collection)")* and the other by Anthony Kerrigan as part of a collaborative translation of the entirety of *Ficciones*.

## Plot

\[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=The_Library_of_Babel&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Plot")\]

Borges' narrator describes how his universe consists of an enormous expanse of adjacent [hexagonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexagon "Hexagon") rooms. In each room, there is an opening in the floor to the hexagons above and below, four walls of bookshelves, and two junctions between hexagons each containing a latrine, a sleeping closet, and a stairwell. Though the order and content of the books are random and apparently completely meaningless, the inhabitants believe that the books contain every possible ordering of just 25 basic [characters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grapheme "Grapheme") (22 letters, the period, the comma, and space). Though the vast majority of the books in this universe are pure [gibberish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gibberish "Gibberish"), the laws of probability dictate that the library also must contain, somewhere, every coherent book ever written, or that might ever be written, and every possible [permutation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permutation "Permutation") or slightly erroneous version of every one of those books. The narrator notes that the library must contain all useful information, including predictions of the future, biographies of any person, and translations of every book in all [languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Language "Language"). Conversely, for many of the texts, some language could be devised that would make it readable with any of a vast number of different contents.

Despite these theories, all books are functionally totally useless to the reader, as any correct, legible text that can exist occurs due to pure chance and must exist alongside countless completely incorrect writings. This leads to many [superstitions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superstition "Superstition"), [cults](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cult "Cult"), and [heresies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heresies "Heresies") within the wider [organized religion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organized_religion "Organized religion") of the library; the "Purifiers" arbitrarily destroy books they deem nonsense as they scour through the library seeking the "Crimson Hexagon" and its illustrated, magical books. Others believe that since all books exist in the library, somewhere one of the books must be a perfect index of the library's contents; some even believe that a [messianic figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Messianic_figure "Messianic figure") known as the "Man of the Book" has read it, and they travel through the library seeking him. The narrator notes the population of the library has been gravely decimated by centuries of religious conflict and disease, but maintains his faith in the beauty and organization of the library as undeniable proof of a [God](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/God "God") or other [demiurge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demiurge "Demiurge"), reaffirming his own attempts to find some ultimate meaning to the library and humanity's existence within it.

## Themes

\[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=The_Library_of_Babel&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Themes")\]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c6/Jorge_Luis_Borges.jpg)

Borges in 1967

The story repeats the theme of Borges' [1939](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1939_in_literature "1939 in literature") essay "[The Total Library](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Total_Library "The Total Library")" ("La Biblioteca Total"), which in turn acknowledges the earlier development of this theme by [Kurd Lasswitz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kurd_Lasswitz "Kurd Lasswitz") in his [1901](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1901_in_literature "1901 in literature") story "The Universal Library" ("Die Universalbibliothek"):

> Certain examples that [Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle "Aristotle") attributes to [Democritus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democritus "Democritus") and [Leucippus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leucippus "Leucippus") clearly prefigure it, but its belated inventor is [Gustav Theodor Fechner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gustav_Theodor_Fechner "Gustav Theodor Fechner"), and its first exponent, [Kurd Lasswitz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kurd_Lasswitz "Kurd Lasswitz"). \[...\] In his book *The Race with the Tortoise* (Berlin, 1919 \[*[sic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sic "Sic")*\]),<sup id="cite_ref-Wolff1929_1-0" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-Wolff1929-1"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup> Dr [Theodor Wolff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theodor_Wolff "Theodor Wolff") suggests that it is a derivation from, or a parody of, [Ramón Llull](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ram%C3%B3n_Llull "Ramón Llull")'s thinking machine \[...\] The elements of his game are the universal orthographic symbols, not the words of a language \[...\] Lasswitz arrives at twenty-five symbols (twenty-two letters, the space, the period, the comma),<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>a<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup> whose recombinations and repetitions encompass everything possible to express in all languages. The totality of such variations would form a Total Library of astronomical size. Lasswitz urges mankind to construct that inhuman library, which chance would organize and which would eliminate intelligence. (Wolff's *The Race with the Tortoise* expounds the execution and the dimensions of that impossible enterprise.)<sup id="cite_ref-Borges1939_5-0" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-Borges1939-5"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup>

Many of Borges' signature motifs are featured in the story, including [infinity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinity "Infinity"), [reality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reality "Reality"), [cabalistic reasoning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kabbalah "Kabbalah"), and [labyrinths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Labyrinth "Labyrinth"). The concept of the library is often compared to [Borel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89mile_Borel "Émile Borel")'s [dactylographic monkey theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinite_monkey_theorem "Infinite monkey theorem"). There is no reference to monkeys or typewriters in "The Library of Babel", although Borges had mentioned that analogy in "The Total Library": "\[A\] half-dozen monkeys provided with typewriters would, in a few eternities, produce all the books in the [British Museum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Museum "British Museum")." In this story, the closest equivalent is the line, "A blasphemous sect suggested \[...\] that all men should juggle letters and symbols until they constructed, by an improbable gift of chance, these canonical books." Borges makes an oblique reference to reproducing Shakespeare, as the only decipherable sentence in one of the books in the library, "O time thy pyramids", is surely taken from the beginning of Shakespeare's [Sonnet 123](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sonnet_123 "Sonnet 123"): "No! Time, thou shalt not boast that I do change / Thy pyramids..."

Borges would examine a similar idea in his 1976 story, "[The Book of Sand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Book_of_Sand "The Book of Sand")", in which there is an infinite book (or book with an indefinite number of pages) rather than an infinite library. Moreover, the story's *Book of Sand* is said to be written in an unknown alphabet and its content is not obviously random. In "The Library of Babel", Borges interpolates Italian mathematician [Bonaventura Cavalieri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bonaventura_Cavalieri "Bonaventura Cavalieri")'s suggestion that any solid body could be conceptualized as the superimposition of an infinite number of planes.

The concept of the library is also overtly analogous to the view of the universe as a [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere") having its center everywhere and its [circumference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumference "Circumference") nowhere. The [mathematician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematician "Mathematician") and [philosopher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosopher "Philosopher") [Blaise Pascal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blaise_Pascal "Blaise Pascal") employed this [metaphor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metaphor "Metaphor"), and in an earlier essay Borges noted that Pascal's manuscript called the sphere *effroyable*, or "frightful".

The quote at the beginning of the story, "By this art you may contemplate the variation of the twenty-three letters", is from [Robert Burton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Burton_\(scholar\) "Robert Burton (scholar)")'s 1621 *[The Anatomy of Melancholy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Anatomy_of_Melancholy "The Anatomy of Melancholy")*.

## Philosophical implications

\[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=The_Library_of_Babel&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Philosophical implications")\]

### Infinite extent

\[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=The_Library_of_Babel&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Infinite extent")\]

In mainstream theories of natural language syntax, every syntactically valid utterance can be extended to produce a new, longer one, because of [recursion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recursion#In_language "Recursion").<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup> However, the books in the Library of Babel are of bounded length ("each book is of four hundred and ten pages; each page, of forty lines, each line, of some eighty letters"), so the Library can only contain a finite number of distinct strings. Borges' narrator notes this fact, but believes that the Library is nevertheless infinite; he speculates that it repeats itself periodically, giving an eventual "order" to the "disorder" of the seemingly random arrangement of books. Mathematics professor William Goldbloom Bloch confirms the narrator's intuition, deducing in his popular mathematics book *[The Unimaginable Mathematics of Borges' Library of Babel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Unimaginable_Mathematics_of_Borges%27_Library_of_Babel "The Unimaginable Mathematics of Borges' Library of Babel")* that the library's structure necessarily has at least one room whose shelves are not full (because the number of books per room does not divide the total number of books evenly), and the rooms on each floor of the library must either be connected into a single [Hamiltonian cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_cycle "Hamiltonian cycle"), or possibly be disconnected into subsets that cannot reach each other.<sup id="cite_ref-hayes_7-0" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-hayes-7"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup>

### Quine's reduction

\[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=The_Library_of_Babel&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Quine's reduction")\]

[W. V. O. Quine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/W._V._O._Quine "W. V. O. Quine") notes that the Library of Babel is finite, and that any text that does not fit in a single book can be reconstructed by finding a second book with the continuation. The size of the alphabet can be reduced by using [Morse code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morse_code "Morse code") even though it makes the books more verbose; the size of the books can also be reduced by splitting each into multiple volumes and discarding the duplicates. Writes Quine, "The ultimate absurdity is now staring us in the face: a universal library of two volumes, one containing a single dot and the other a dash. Persistent repetition and alternation of the two are sufficient, we well know, for spelling out any and every truth. The miracle of the finite but universal library is a mere inflation of the miracle of binary notation: everything worth saying, and everything else as well, can be said with two characters."<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup>

## Comparison with biology

\[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=The_Library_of_Babel&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Comparison with biology")\]

The full possible set of protein sequences ([protein sequence space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sequence_space_\(evolution\) "Sequence space (evolution)")) has been compared to the Library of Babel.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup> In the *Library of Babel*, finding any book that made sense was almost impossible due to the sheer number and lack of order. The same would be true of protein sequences if it were not for natural selection, which has picked out only protein sequences that make sense. Additionally, each protein sequence is surrounded by a set of neighbors (point mutants) that are likely to have at least some function. [Daniel Dennett](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daniel_Dennett "Daniel Dennett")'s 1995 book *[Darwin's Dangerous Idea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Darwin%27s_Dangerous_Idea "Darwin's Dangerous Idea")* includes an elaboration of the Library of Babel concept to imagine the set of all possible genetic sequences, which he calls the Library of Mendel, in order to illustrate the mathematics of [genetic variation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetic_variation "Genetic variation"). Dennett uses this concept again later in the book to imagine all possible algorithms that can be included in his [Toshiba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toshiba "Toshiba") computer, which he calls the Library of Toshiba. He describes the Library of Mendel and the Library of Toshiba as subsets within the Library of Babel.

## Influence on later works

\[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=The_Library_of_Babel&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Influence on later works")\]

- [Érik Desmazières](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89rik_Desmazi%C3%A8res "Érik Desmazières") provided etched illustrations for a 1997 edition of the short story, depicting the library as literally built in the shape of [Bruegel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pieter_Bruegel_the_Elder "Pieter Bruegel the Elder")'s [*Tower of Babel*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Tower_of_Babel_\(Bruegel\) "The Tower of Babel (Bruegel)").
- [Umberto Eco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umberto_Eco "Umberto Eco")'s postmodern novel *[The Name of the Rose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Name_of_the_Rose "The Name of the Rose")* (1980) features a labyrinthine library, presided over by a blind monk named Jorge of Burgos. The room is, however, octagonal in shape.
- Russell Standish's *Theory of Nothing*<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup> uses the concept of the Library of Babel to illustrate how an [ultimate ensemble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultimate_ensemble "Ultimate ensemble") containing all possible descriptions would in sum contain zero information and would thus be the simplest possible explanation for the existence of the universe. This theory, therefore, implies the reality of all universes.
- *[The Unimaginable Mathematics of Borges' Library of Babel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Unimaginable_Mathematics_of_Borges%27_Library_of_Babel "The Unimaginable Mathematics of Borges' Library of Babel")* (2008) by [William Goldbloom Bloch](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=William_Goldbloom_Bloch&action=edit&redlink=1 "William Goldbloom Bloch (page does not exist)") explores the short story from a mathematical perspective. Bloch analyzes the hypothetical library presented by Borges using the ideas of [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology"), [information theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_theory "Information theory"), and geometry.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup>
- In [Greg Bear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greg_Bear "Greg Bear")'s novel *[City at the End of Time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/City_at_the_End_of_Time "City at the End of Time")* (2008), the sum-runners carried by the protagonists are intended by their creator to be combined to form a 'Babel', an infinite library containing every possible permutation of every possible character in every possible language. Bear has stated that this was inspired by Borges, who is also namechecked in the novel. Borges is described as an unknown Argentinian who commissioned an encyclopedia of impossible things, a reference to either "[Tlön, Uqbar, Orbis Tertius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tl%C3%B6n,_Uqbar,_Orbis_Tertius "Tlön, Uqbar, Orbis Tertius")" or the *[Book of Imaginary Beings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Book_of_Imaginary_Beings "Book of Imaginary Beings")*.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2023)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
- [The Library of Babel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Library_of_Babel_\(website\) "The Library of Babel (website)"), a website created by [Jonathan Basile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jonathan_Basile "Jonathan Basile"), emulates an English-language version of Borges' library. An algorithm he created generates a "book" by iterating every permutation of 29 characters: the 26 English letters, space, comma, and period. Each book is marked by a coordinate, corresponding to its place on the hexagonal library (hexagon name, wall number, shelf number, and book name) so that every book can be found at the same place every time. The website is said to contain "all possible pages of 3,200 characters, about 10<sup>4677</sup> books".<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup> For example, a coordinate may look like "389fj39l-w4-s5-v32" where, "389fj39l" is the hexagon name, "w4" specifies wall 4, "s5" specifies shelf 5, and "v32" specifies volume 32. This has inspired similar projects for [images](https://babelia.libraryofbabel.info/about.html) (permuting 4,096 colours onto a grid of 416×640 pixels) and [audio](https://library-of-babel-audio.vercel.app/library) (permuting one-second audio clips at a quality of 44.1 kHz and 16 bits).
- In [Steven L. Peck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steven_L._Peck "Steven L. Peck")'s novella *[A Short Stay In Hell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Short_Stay_in_Hell "A Short Stay in Hell")* (2009), the protagonist must find the book of his life's story in a library containing every possible book. Borges' story is mentioned directly, although the library is structured very differently. It is also explicitly finite in size, though it is more than a million [orders of magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orders_of_magnitude "Orders of magnitude") larger than the [observable universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe "Observable universe").
- Turkish media artist [Refik Anadol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refik_Anadol "Refik Anadol") created the installation *Archive Dreaming* (2017), inspired by Borges' The Library of Babel. Anadol's work employs machine learning algorithms to create an immersive, interactive digital archive, visually exploring infinite combinations of data and memory, thus reflecting Borges' concepts of infinite knowledge, randomness, and order.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup>
- *[Library of Ruina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Library_of_Ruina "Library of Ruina")* (2021) is a South Korean deck-building video game whose titular location is heavily inspired by the Library of Babel. People defeated in combat within the library are turned into books that can be manipulated in various means, which leads to the protagonist Angela trying to find the "One True Book".
- DC Comics [Justice League (2018)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Justice_League_\(2018\)&action=edit&redlink=1 "Justice League (2018) (page does not exist)") Issue 60-63 makes reference to the Library of Babel. The Justice League Dark members enter the fictional world of the book to retrieve Merlin's spellbook (as it is a library that has every book in existence).
- The world of *Black Beacon* (2024), a Chinese [ARPG](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_role-playing_game "Action role-playing game"), takes much inspiration from the Library of Babel. In the game, the Babel Library is an endless labyrinth formed through infinite permutations based on the space within the Babel Tower. "The library holds everything; it is the sum of all things our languages can express."<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-16"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup>
